NARRATOR

Miller was captivated by the poetry of Minnie Dyer, the "Sweet Singer of the Coquille", and wrote to her.  After receiving her reply, a scholarly criticism of verses he had submitted to her, Miller impulsively rode to Port Orford and meeting Minnie on the Dyer porch proposed to her.

 

MARBERRY

Joaquin literally overpowered Minnie Myrtle and, for that matter, her steady beau too, the man she intended to marry.  The day after Joaquin arrived unannounced and uninvited at Port Orford this rival came to visit his fiancée.  Joaquin was outraged.  He drew his revolver and drove the bewildered young man from the house.

 

NARRATOR

Hiner and Minnie were married three days later.

LILLIE LANGTREE

I went to a concert at Lady Brassey's, and at the foot of the broad staircase stood Joaquin Miller . . . . as I walked up-stairs to greet my hostess, he backed before me, scattering rose-leaves (which he had concealed in his sombrero) upon the white-marble steps and saying with fervor, "This be your path in life."

 

JOAQUIN

Come, listen O Love to the voice of the dove, Come, hearken and hear him say, "There are many Tomorrows, my Love, my Love, There is only one Today."

JOAQUIN

In men whom men condemn as ill I find so much of goodness still, In men whom men pronounce divine I find so much of sin and blot, I hesitate to draw a line Between the two, where God has not.

JOAQUIN

They sailed and sailed, as winds might blow, Until at last the blanched mate said; "Why, now not even God would know Should I and all my men fall dead. These very winds forget their way, For God from these dread seas is gone.Now speak, brave Adm'r'l; speak and say" - He said: "Sail on! sail on! and on!"
