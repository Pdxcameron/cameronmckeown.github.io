JOAQUIN


Rifles cracked around the camp, and arrows came back in showers

. . . .we left cover and advanced.  I think I must have swallowed the bullets I put in my mouth, for I loaded from my pouch as usual, and thought of them no more as we moved down upon the yelling Indians. Suddenly the arrows, whiz, whistle, thud, right in our faces. I fell senseless.

 
The fight was over.  An arrow had struck me in the left side of the face, struck the jaw-bone, and then glanced around and came out at the back of the neck . . . .
 
We had no surgeon, and the men tied up our wounds as best they could in tobacco saturated in saliva . . . .
 

MARBERRY
[H]e was confined in a cell with a notorious badman, one Jack Marshall.
The jail was flimsy and the two men easily sawed through the bars with a pocketknife . . . . [T]hey fled to secluded Scott Valley.
 

HAIGHT

Then he lived on an island in Scott's valley with [a] notorious band, - Jack Marshall, Nels Scott, Dave English and Frank Thompkins.
 

JOAQUIN

I made off up the mountain, perhaps fifty yards in advance of the horsemen, and on foot. 

Pistols flourished in the air, the men started forward almost upon me

.. . . The hillside was steep and rocky, and the mettlesome little Mexican horses refused to rush upon me across the steep and broken ground, but began to spin round like tops, and would not advance up the hill.

Some hard, iron-clad oats, and then shot after shot

. . . . The officer was unhorsed, and lay bleeding on the ground from a frightful wound, while Hirst [sic], further down the hill could only fire random shots over the head of his restless and plunging horse . . . .
 

NARRATOR

Most Deadwood salons competed in claiming that the bullet each displayed was the one that Joaquin used to shoot Constable Sion Bradley.
 

MARBERRY
Joaquin set up a law office in Canyon City and, if he did not make much money . . . he won the esteem of his fellow citizens.
 

JOAQUIN

If the shrewd and sharp lawyers who bullied and defeated me had come into my elements I would have beaten them.  But I had chosen to enter theirs, and must be equal to the undertaking.  And so it was I worked and studied as never man worked and studied before. 

Often I never left my office till the gray dawn, after a day of toil and a night of study.  My health gave way and I was indeed old and thoughtful.
 

MARBERRY
. . . . Joaquin was elected Captain of a company of fifty-four volunteers sent to aid the U.S. Army in fighting the marauders . . . . The U.S. Cavalry . . . pursued the Indians, who were well armed and far superior in strength, south of the Harney Lake region.  There, because they were so outnumbered, the soldiers hesitated to engage in the fight.  At this juncture Captain Miller and his volunteers came riding down the trail.  The battle started immediately.  There was a comic touch when one volunteer received a scratch wound only to find seven members of the Miller company dismount, throw down their weapons and carry him, and themselves, safely from the field; but Joaquin's conduct as an officer was irreproachable.
 

SURVIVOR
. . . . tied his hat to the saddle and his long hair streaming about his head, led the charge on a mule against the Indians fortified on a ridge.  Joaquin's mule was shot out from under him and he continued up the slope on foot.  The Indians retired from the battlefield, with the cavalry chasing them down, and that was the end of the war.
